Topology-Aware Federated Learning for
Cyber-Physical Intrusion Detection in DER
Muhammad Hamza Karim, Derrick Agyapong, Bo Tu, Prakash Ranganathan
Center for Cyber Security Research (C2SR)
University of North Dakota, Grand Forks, North Dakota, USA
{muhammad.karim, derrick.agyapong, bo.tu, prakash.ranganathan}@und.edu
Abstract—The integration of Distributed Energy Resources
(DERs) transforms traditional distribution grids into dynamic
cyber-physical systems (CPS). However, the constant fluctuations
in power generation caused by DERs make it easier for stealthy
temporal cyberattacks, such as Replay and Denial of Service
(DoS), to remain hidden within normal grid behavior. While
Federated Learning (FL) provides privacy-preserving intrusion
detection, existing models are fundamentally topology-blind;
they evaluate edge nodes in isolation and fail to enforce
underlying electrical constraints. To address this vulnerability,
this
paper
proposes
a
Topology-Aware
Federated
Edge
Learning framework. To simulate a distribution network we
utilized IEEE 33-Bus network augmented with active solar
DERs, the grid is geographically partitioned into distinct
zones. Localized LSTM Autoencoders are deployed to edge
clients to learn the temporal dynamics of physical states
(Active Power, Reactive Power, Voltage Magnitude, and Phase
Angle). A central server aggregates these models to enforce
spatial-temporal consistency across the entire topology without
centralizing raw operational data. By cross-validating physical
constraints between neighboring zones, the proposed architecture
successfully detects stealthy temporal manipulations that easily
bypass isolated localized systems.

I. INTRODUCTION
Historically, electrical distribution networks operated as
passive one-way systems that delivered power from substations
to consumers. However, the rapid growth of Distributed
Energy Resources (DERs) has changed this structure. The grid
now behaves as a dynamic bidirectional cyber-physical system
(CPS) [1]. Although this shift enables decentralized energy
management, it also introduces instability. Weather conditions
such as sunlight, wind, and temperature directly influence
power generation, causing continuous and unpredictable
changes in power flow. This creates a noisy and complex
operating
environment,
making
it
harder
to
separate
normal behavior from attacks and ultimately increasing the
cyber-physical attack surface.
This inherent variability creates an ideal environment for
sophisticated cyberattacks to remain undetected. Although
conventional False Data Injection Attacks (FDIAs) often
focus on manipulating sensor values directly, more advanced
temporal attacks such as replay attacks exploit the timing
and continuity of time-series data, making them significantly
harder to detect within naturally volatile DER environments
[9]. An attacker can record legitimate grid measurements
during low-demand periods and replay them during peak
demand to conceal malicious actions. Because the injected
values remain within normal ranges, standard threshold-based
detectors do not raise alarms. Instead, the attack takes
advantage of the natural variability of DERs. From an isolated
edge node’s perspective, a replayed active power signal can be
indistinguishable from natural fluctuations caused by weather
variations such as cloud cover [10].
To secure decentralized grid infrastructure while preserving
the privacy of sensitive operational data, researchers have
increasingly adopted Federated Learning (FL) frameworks
[11], [12]. However, existing FL-based intrusion detection
methods remain largely topology-blind. They model DERs as
independent entities and lack the spatial awareness needed to
enforce physical constraints such as Kirchhoff’s voltage and
current laws across multi-node distribution networks [13]. As
a result, temporally consistent but malicious signals can evade
detection at the local level, even when they violate the physical
consistency of the broader system.
To bridge the gap between IT-focused anomaly detection
and
OT-level
physical
constraint
enforcement,
this
paper proposes the Intelligent Network Topology-Aware
Collaborative Training (INTACT) framework. We utilized
the IEEE 33-Bus distribution system [14] to simulate a
neighborhood-level grid topology, specifically modeling the
integration of active solar DERs to capture the complex
dynamics of bidirectional power flow. The grid is partitioned
into operational zones based on electrical proximity, where
each zone is monitored by a localized edge computing client
that trains a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Autoencoder
on its local physical state variables: Active Power (P),
Reactive Power (Q), Voltage Magnitude (|V |) and Phase
Angle (θ). Rather than centralizing raw grid data, zonal
clients periodically transmit model weights to a federated
aggregation server. Unlike standard Federated Averaging
(FedAvg) [15], which treats all clients equally, our server
assigns higher aggregation weight to zones that are more
strongly connected in the physical network, measured by
the admittance of the lines between them. This ensures
that electrically coupled zones have greater influence on
each other’s learned model, reflecting the real physical
relationships of the grid. Additionally, after each round
of aggregation, the server compares reconstruction errors
between neighboring zones. Since real physical disturbances

II. RELATED WORK
Research

that prioritize communication privacy and physics-informed

A. Federated Learning for Grid Intrusion Detection
Most FL approaches established the viability of distributed
anomaly detection without centralizing the raw operational
data. Purohit et al. [2] proposed FL-ADS, a federated anomaly
detection system for DER networks targeting communication
protocols such as DNP3 and Modbus. While the system
effectively coordinates distributed clients, it secures the
communication layer without modeling physical power flow
variables, remaining blind to the physical coupling structure of
the underlying network. Kausar et al. [3] developed a federated
deep learning model using physical measurements for FDIA
detection in cyber-physical power systems, yet do not isolate
the vulnerability shift introduced when dynamic solar DERs
are added to radial grids, leaving the impact of bidirectional
power flow on detection performance unquantified. Kesici et
al. [4] addressed data partitioning through vertical federated
learning on the IEEE 123-bus and IEEE 37-node systems,
achieving strong FDIA detection using various sub-network
measurements.
While
the
spatial-temporal
split
learning
architecture represents a step toward physical awareness, the
work focuses on static network partitioning and does not
examine how the transition from passive unidirectional grids
to active DER-integrated networks reshapes the detection
landscape.

III. GRID SIMULATION AND THREAT MODELING
A. IEEE 33-Bus Simulation Setup
The IEEE 33-Bus radial distribution system [16] is used as
the simulation testbed. This benchmark network consists of a
single feeder originating from a main feeder/slack bus (Bus 0,
fixed at 1.0 pu) that supplies 32 load buses through a radial
topology, making it representative of a neighborhood-level
distribution network. All simulations are conducted using
PandaPower [17], an open source power flow simulator.
To simulate DER integration, three solar photovoltaic units
were placed at Bus 17 (0.5 MW), Bus 24 (0.4 MW), and
Bus 32 (0.6 MW) across different network branches. Solar
generation follows a normal weather-driven profile at 5-minute
intervals and operates only between 06:00 and 20:00. The
simulation covers four weeks, generating 8,064 timesteps per
bus. At each timestep, four key physical variables are recorded:
Active Power (P), Reactive Power (Q), Voltage Magnitude
(|V |), and Phase Angle (θ). Two simulation scenarios are
generated under identical load profiles and weather conditions:
• No-DER baseline: DER output is set to zero at all
three injection buses, representing a passive unidirectional
distribution network.
• DER-active scenario: All three solar units operate
normally, introducing bidirectional power flow during
solar generation hours.
The grid is divided into four operational zones based on
electrical proximity and feeder structure, as defined in Table II.

B. Grid Comparison With & Without DER
To measure the impact of DER integration on the operating
environment of anomaly detection systems, the two simulation
scenarios are compared across voltage magnitude and active
power dimensions. Fig. 1(a) shows the average daily active
power difference (∆P) at each DER bus, computed as
the mean difference between the DER-active and No-DER
scenarios across all 28 simulated days. During the solar
window, all three DER buses exhibit strongly negative ∆P,
with peak injections of −0.6 MW (Bus 32), −0.5 MW
(Bus 17), and −0.4 MW (Bus 24) at approximately 12:00.
Outside the solar window, ∆P
=
0 across all buses,
confirming that the two scenarios are physically identical at
night.
The effect on the voltage distribution is shown in Fig. 1(b).
The No-DER distribution is narrow and concentrated near the
lower operational limit (0.95 pu), reflecting the predictable
voltage droop of a passive radial feeder. The DER-active
distribution is significantly wider and shifted toward higher
voltages, spanning from 0.93 pu to 1.00 pu. A Welch’s
t-test [18] confirms this difference is statistically significant
(t = 227.9, p < 0.001), with DER integration raising the mean
system voltage by +0.008 pu and reducing voltage violations
below 0.95 pu by 68.8% (from 44,552 to 13,920 timesteps
across the full system). This increased distributional spread
elevates the baseline reconstruction noise floor for locally
trained anomaly detectors, creating the operating environment
in which temporally consistent replay attacks can exploit
natural variability to evade detection.

D. Stealthy Replay Attack Design
In a naive replay attack, an attacker substitutes real-time
measurements with historical data via a hard cut. However, this
introduces sudden temporal changes that localized time-series
anomaly detectors, such as LSTM autoencoders, can easily
identify. To rigorously evaluate the INTACT framework, we
design a stealthy context-aware replay attack that remains
consistent with local grid operating conditions while exploiting
the spatial blind spot of zone-isolated detection.
Let the cyber-physical state of a zone at time t be the
complete multi-variable measurement vector:
y(t) = [P(t), Q(t), |V (t)|, θ(t)]T
(1)
All four physical state variables — active power P, reactive
power Q, voltage magnitude |V |, and phase angle θ —
are simultaneously replaced at every timestep of the attack
window. Full multi-variable substitution preserves the internal
AC power flow consistency of the attacked zone: because all
four variables are jointly replaced with historically coherent
values, the local LSTM reconstructs the injected sequence with
low residual e(t) = ∥y(t)−ˆy(t)∥2 ≤τth, preventing detection
by the isolated edge node.
The attack is executed in two steps:
• Statistical Window Selection: Rather than replaying
arbitrary historical data, the attacker identifies a past time
window whose statistical properties — mean and standard
deviation across all features — closely match the target
window [19]. For DER-equipped zones (Zones 2–4),
targets are peak solar generation periods (10:00–14:00);
for Zone 1 (load-only), targets are evening peak load
periods (18:00–20:00). Source windows are constrained
to differ by more than two hours from the target hour and
to originate at least three days prior, preventing trivial
same-hour matching in the synthetic dataset.
• Cross-Fade
Boundary
Blending:
To
mask
the
injection boundary, sensor readings are cross-faded
over 12 timesteps (60 minutes) at each injection edge,
smoothly transitioning from real-time to historical values
and back [20]. Only the unblended middle portion is

E. Evaluation Dataset and Attack Injection Strategy
Stealthy replay attacks are injected independently into the
test data of each zone: three events in Zone 1 and four events
each in Zones 2–4, with each event spanning a 4-hour window
(48 timesteps at 5-minute resolution). Each local model is
evaluated exclusively on its own zone’s attacked test file,
which contains only the 32–36 bus-level features belonging
to that zone.
For centralized and FL model evaluation, a combined test
dataset is constructed by merging all four zone test files
column-wise (128 features total). The global attack label is set
to 1 whenever any zone is under attack, yielding 631 attack
windows out of 4,002 total (15.8%). This evaluation protocol
tests whether centralized and FL models can exploit cross-zone
spatial correlations that are invisible to any single local model,
providing a direct measure of the benefit conferred by global
situational awareness.
IV. ISOLATED AND CENTRALIZED DETECTION BASELINES
We evaluate two baseline architectures to benchmark our
INTACT framework. The first is an isolated local LSTM
Autoencoder. Here, each model trains only on data from its
own zone. The second is a centralized LSTM Autoencoder.
This represents the theoretical upper bound with full data
access. For both setups, the input sequences span T = 30
consecutive timesteps (2.5 hours). They cover four physical
state variables per bus: P, Q, |V |, and θ.
A. Decentralized Local Autoencoder Training
The local configuration trains one LSTM Autoencoder per
zone. It uses only that zone’s measurement data. Zones do
not share any information during training or inference. This
mimics a scenario where each edge node operates as a
completely isolated anomaly detector.
We ensured a mathematically fair baseline by optimizing
each model independently. We used the Optuna framework
for this step [22]. This allowed every local model to find
its optimal architecture, such as hidden units and learning
rates, tailored to its specific DER variance. Next, we calculated
independent anomaly detection thresholds (τth) for each zone.
These were set at a high percentile of the local training
reconstruction errors, ranging from the 99.1th to the 99.9th
percentile based on local noise profiles.
During testing, any reconstruction error above the localized
τth triggers an alarm. Table III shows how each zone
performed against the stealthy replay attack.
Detection performance varies widely across the zones. This
reflects the diverse operating conditions caused by DER
integration. Zone 4 hosts the largest DER unit (0.6 MW at
Bus 32). It achieves the highest recall at 0.94. This happens

B. Centralized ML Model Training
The centralized baseline takes a different approach. It trains
a single LSTM Autoencoder on the combined raw data from
all four zones at once. The input space spans 128 features at
each timestep. This gives the model complete visibility over
the entire physical system.
This
model
processes
a
much
larger
feature
space.
Therefore, we ran a dedicated Optuna search on the combined
dataset to find the optimal global architecture. The best setup
used two stacked LSTM layers (32 and 64 units), no dropout,
and a learning rate of 5.73 × 10−3. We set the anomaly
threshold at the 99.4th percentile (τth = 0.0046) of the
combined training errors. We must ensure a fair comparison
with the federated models later. Therefore, we locked this
exact global architecture and threshold percentile for all
subsequent Federated Learning (FL) experiments.

exact tradeoff motivates our INTACT framework. We want to
replicate the high detection rates of central awareness, but keep
the per-zone data locality to prevent unnecessary false alarms.
